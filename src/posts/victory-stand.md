## Plot summary 
What happened in Tommie Smith’s life before he raised his fist at the 1968 Olympics? This graphic memoir tells his story, moving from his childhood on a Texas farm to high school in California’s Central Valley to San José State University, where he trains to become the world’s fastest runner. In “Speed City,” he meets other Black athletes who are putting San Jose on the world stage while experiencing discrimination and learning about the Black Power movement growing across the country. Smith finds his role in the movement as he gets stronger and faster on the track.

## Author background
Tommie Smith is a track and field athlete known for raising his fist on the 1968 Olympic podium. Banned from competition for this salute to Black Power, he was belatedly inducted into the U.S. National Track & Field Hall of Fame and won the 2008 Arthur Ashe Courage Award (Britannica, 2026). Smith published his autobiography, _Silent Gesture_, in 2007.


Derrick Barnes has written more than a dozen children’s books, including _Crown: An Ode to the Fresh Cut_, which won Caldecott, Newberry, and Coretta Scott King honors. A father of four boys, he aims “to paint positive, beautiful pictures of African American children, to tell positive, beautiful stories about my babies” (McCutcheon, 2018).

Dawud Anyabwile is a comic book illustrator, animator, and fine artist known for the _Brotherman: Dictator of Discipline_ series (Degand, 2022).

## Critical evaluation
This graphic narrative took me into Smith’s world, conveying the terror of growing up amidst Jim Crow segregation and KKK violence; the love he received from his sister, present at every track meet; and the exhilaration of proving, again and again, that he was the best at his sport. Anyabwile’s black-and-white illustrations range from a close-up panel of a competitor’s side-eye to a full page of Smith, gloriously breaking through finish-line tape. Smith and Barnes employ historical reportage, second-by-second descriptions of the final Olympic race, and first-person narration of Smith’s growth, skillfully building narrative tension even though we know he ends up on the podium. I recommend this read even if you don’t usually like history books, and even if you typically like the football field more than the library.

## Library program
_Write your own graphic memoir._ Teens are invited to write personal stories in comic form. They may create simple two-panel comics or longer zines. Provide a variety of paper, drawing and coloring utensils, and if possible, scrap material such as magazines weeded from a library’s collection. Encourage teens not to stress about their drawing skills. Stick figures can tell a story! Provide prompts related to the book, such as:

* Write about your childhood home
* Write about someone who always cheers you on
* Write about a time that you felt proud or triumphant
* Write about a person or conversation that inspired you
* Write about an experience that felt unfair
* Write about a time when you weren’t sure what you were going to do

## Speed-round book talk
You’ve probably seen the famous photograph. Two Black track-and-field athletes, Tommie Smith and John Carlos, stand on an Olympic podium with heads bowed and fists raised. This graphic memoir is your chance to read Smith’s story in a format packed with as much action and emotion as your favorite superhero comic.

## Potential challenges
Books on Black history are often challenged, especially when Black characters dare to defend themselves. _Victory. Stand!_ shows Smith as he develops his politics, one time striking an elementary school classmate after the classmate makes a racist insult and later, organizing with Black Panthers who espouse revolution.

Both racist terror and the multiplicity of responses to it are facts that have shaped the United States, and librarians should defend readers’ rights to access such historical depictions.

## Reason for inclusion
This book does so many good things. It shows readers the history of sharecropping in the American South, of Black migration from Texas and Oklahoma to California, and of the civil rights and Black Power movements of the 1960s. It depicts young people finding their role in society while historic events unfold around them. It shows individual acts of courage alongside cooperation and collective action; it shows what difference a supportive person like Coach Winter can make. It’s educational, and it’s triumphant.
