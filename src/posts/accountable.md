## Awards and honors
* Winner, YALSA Excellence in Nonfiction for Young Adults, [2024](https://www.ala.org/news/2011/11/accountable-true-story-racist-social-media-account-and-teenagers-whose-lives-it)

## Reading level and interest level
Grades 9 to 12, according to NoveList.

## Plot summary 
In 2017, a Korean American student posted racist images on a private Instagram account to make his friends laugh. _Accountable_ covers the reverberations felt by students, teachers, parents, and their entire small, Bay Area town after the memes are discovered. Albany High School administrators try to hold the Instagram’s creator and followers to account while helping both the “harmers” and the “impacted” complete their high school educations. The author surveys court documents and interviews both the followers and the Black girls who found their pictures on the page.

## Author background
Dashka Slater is the author of _The 57 Bus_ and more than a dozen other children’s, teen, and adult books (University of San Francisco, n.d.).

Ariel Blake is a performing artist, filmmaker, teacher, poet, and doula (Blake, n.d.). She narrated _Luster_ and _An Abolitionist’s Handbook._

## Critical evaluation
When Slater started reporting this story, “100 percent of the people involved did not want to talk about it,” she said (Mechanic, 2023). And in that sense, _Accountable_ is an impressive feat of journalism. Slater got students to talk about some of the worst days of their lives, and as they spoke, it hit me how connected everyone was. The Black girls harassed on the account had considered some of its followers to be their friends. One talked about running into Charles, the creator of the account, on their small town’s main business strip. Slater weaves in court documents and historical context, reminding us that as the teens tried to go back to school, white supremacists rallied in Charlottesville, and an uprising swept the country in response to George Floyd’s murder.

I usually hate true crime. Revenge and carceral logic flourish in the genre. _Accountable_ is more nuanced, presenting balanced perspectives and ideas of restorative and transformative justice. The audiobook is narrated in an even, measured tone. When the account’s most unrepentant follower defends “racial difference” as “science,” Slater uses research to refute his ideas. 

Still, I would have a hard time recommending this book to Black teens, or really anyone familiar with the everyday racism of American life. The book drags in the second half as it goes into the account followers’ mental suffering. I don’t believe in harsh juvenile punishment, or really punitive justice at all, but did we really need several chapters exploring their shame? Is fact-checking the appropriate response when an interviewee spews “race science”? While white and Asian boys’ mistakes are handled with compassion, Slater describes Black girls as “angry more often than hurt” (Mel the Bookworm, 2023) and compares them to trains and powder kegs. 

_Accountable_ is a candid report that might serve as a tool in conversations about race, internet culture, and high school boys’ social dynamics. Teachers, librarians, parents, and teens interested in education, psychology, or law might find it a useful case study. But Slater follows misguided journalistic norms, balancing two sides of a story that was never fair to begin with. She could have explored more thoroughly the gendered nature of the harassment and the culpability of social media corporations. If you’re a teen want to read about ethics and varying forms of justice, I recommend <a href="{{ '/luis-ortega-survival-club/' | url }}"><em>The Luis Ortega Survival Club</em></a> or <a href="{{ '/victory-stand/' | url }}"><em>Victory. Stand!</em></a>; if you’re a teen trying to navigate a racist school, I recommend <a href="{{ '/babel' | url }}"><em>Babel</em></a> or <a href="{{ '/estela-undrowning/' | url }}"><em>Estela, Undrowning.</em></a>

## Library program
_Types of justice._ This book doesn’t lend itself to any activity other than serious discussion. Play the chapter of the audiobook where Slater describes four types of justice, then discuss the questions below. Prepare for active facilitation: Ensure that students feel safe and no one student dominates the conversation.

* Slater describes four types of justice: punitive, permissive, restorative, and transformative. What comes to mind when you hear the word justice? Are there other types? What types of justice do you most often see in your communities?
* On March 30, 2017, the school attempted a mediation session. Setting aside the issue of the sit-in protest, what could the mediators have done better inside the room?
* What have you learned in school about racism? What do you think high schools should teach students about race? What about elementary schools and middle schools?
* Near the end of the book, Slater talks about social media algorithms, and how they favor likes, comments, and shares, regardless of the content of a post. In your experience, what types of content go viral? What responsibility do you think social media companies have to regulate the content on their platforms?

## Speed-round book talk
In 2017, a Korean American teenager started posting anti-Black memes on an Instagram account to make his friends laugh. _Accountable_ tells the story of the fallout using court documents and interviews with the account’s creator, its followers, and the students they harmed. The book is not an easy read, and it sometimes reproduces the white liberal bias present in its small Bay Area town setting, but it opens important conversations about racism, teen social dynamics, and internet culture.

## Potential challenges
_Accountable_ has been removed from two school districts for its racial and political content (Banned Books, 2026). But censorship will not stop young people from learning about racism nor from seeking racial justice. One student in the book, after getting harassed on the Instagram account, started a program for high school volunteers to talk with middle schoolers about race. This type of education is necessary for young people learning to navigate U.S. society and culture, and _Accountable_ helps facilitate it. 

## Reason for inclusion
I originally chose this book because it’s an award-winning title about events that took place not far from my home. I also wanted to include a piece of reported nonfiction. Now that I’ve read the book, I’d say it’s a useful document that belongs in a well-rounded teen collection, but I’d caution that it’s not for everyone.
