## Plot summary 
Francisco Maghabol is an Ilocano teenager who leaves the Philippines after his father deserts their family. He finds work in Watsonville, California, but he soon finds that he can’t make enough to send money home, let alone to return to the Philippines himself. He stays, eventually becoming a leader in the 1960s farmworker movement. The book tells his story alongside three generations of Maghobol stories that follow.

Emil feels abandoned by a father who’s never home and who seems only to care about labor organizing. He studies hard, hoping to build a career far from the Stockton diner where he grows up. After finding success as an engineer, he’s strict with his son, Chris, who’s doesn’t understand his father. Chris wants to play football and have fun like the other teens. When he starts learning about his Filipino heritage for a school assignment, Emil is uninterested, reminding Chris that they’re American, and that history books won’t make him any money.

In 2020, Emil, Chris, and his son Enzo are stuck in a house together. Enzo is overwhelmed and anxious, but he also has an opportunity. He’s the first in his family to be able to have a real conversation with his grandfather — that is, if Lolo Emil abides.

## Author background
Randy Ribay is a teacher and author (Asian Pacific American Librarians Association, 2020). His novel _Patron Saints of Nothing_ was a finalist for the National Book Award in 2019. He was born in the Philippines and raised in the Midwest.

## Critical evaluation
I’m thoroughly impressed by this work, which weaves research and prose to create characters with just the right amount of historical specificity. Ribay takes us from one Maghabol’s perspective to the next, layering nuance behind each impulsive act, every headstrong argument. I got frustrated with every character, and in turn, forgave him. By the end of the novel, the Maghabols felt like family.

While Ribay primarily explores masculinity and father-son relationships, the women and girls are powerful characters in their own right. The audiobook is read by four different narrators of the Filipino diaspora, including actor Manny Jacinto. They intone an appropriate level of drama and humor, but the narration is best in moments of quiet inner dialogue. Even Enzo — whose story I found to be the least interesting perhaps because his contemporary anxieties are too similar to mine — won me over as he fought the “murder hornets” in his head to connect with his best friend and his family.

## Library program
_Father’s Day brunch and book club._ Teens are encouraged to read the book alongside their fathers before the event. Four tables are set up, one for every Maghabol protagonist. At each table, a Filipino or American dish has been arranged with a printed passage from the book and discussion questions. Let each table discuss before sharing their thoughts with the rest of the attendees.
