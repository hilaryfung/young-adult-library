Age 12 and up, according to Penguin Random House (n.d.). 

## Plot summary 
Francisco Maghabol is an Ilocano teenager who leaves the Philippines after his father deserts their family. He finds work in Watsonville, California, but he soon finds that he can’t make enough to send money home, let alone to return to the Philippines himself. He stays, eventually becoming a leader in the 1960s farmworker movement. The novel tells his story alongside three generations of Maghabol stories that follow.

Emil feels abandoned by a father who’s never home and who seems only to care about labor organizing. He studies hard, hoping to build a career far from the Stockton diner where he works. After finding success as an engineer, he’s strict with his son, Chris, who mostly wants to play football and have fun like the other teens.

Fast-forward to the COVID-19 pandemic, and Emil, Chris, and his son Enzo are stuck together in a house in Philadelphia. Enzo feels overwhelmed and anxious, but he also has an opportunity. He’s the first in his family to be able to have a real conversation with his grandfather — that is, if Lolo Emil abides.

## Author and narrator backgrounds
Randy Ribay is a teacher and author (Asian Pacific American Librarians Association, 2020). His novel _Patron Saints of Nothing_ was a finalist for the National Book Award in 2019. He was born in the Philippines and raised in the Midwest.

Ramón de Ocampo is a television and theater actor known for his role on _The West Wing_ (IMDb, n.d.).

Jesse Inocalla is the Filipino American voice actor behind Soren in _The Dragon Prince_ (Vancouver Acting School, n.d.)

Manny Jacinto is a Canadian actor from Quezon City (Cabana, 2024). He played Jason Mendoza on _The Good Place_.

JB Tadena is a child of Filipino immigrants known for his roles in _Hawaii Five-0_, _NCIS: Los Angeles_, and _Kung Fu_ (Tadena, n.d.; Lewis, 2023).


## Critical evaluation
I’m thoroughly impressed by this work, which weaves research and prose to create historical characters with just the right amount of specificity. Ribay takes us from one Maghabol’s perspective to the next, layering nuance behind each impulsive act, every headstrong argument. I got frustrated with each character, and in turn, forgave him. By the end of the novel, the Maghabols felt like family. 

While Ribay primarily explores masculinity and father-son relationships, the women and girls are powerful characters in their own right. Ironically, I thought the one story that lacked historical color was Enzo’s. One could argue that he was stuck in place — not that much could really happen to him at home — and perhaps I was put off by contemporary anxieties too similar to my own. But 2020 was an eventful year. Philadelphia police killed Walter Wallace in October (Conde, 2020); even before that, Enzo could not have lived too far from a protest. That his story developed mostly through events on his phone felt strange to me.

The audiobook is read by four different narrators of the Filipino diaspora, including actor Manny Jacinto. They intone an appropriate level of drama and humor, but the narration is best in moments of quiet inner dialogue. In the end, Enzo still won me over as he fought the “murder hornets” in his head to connect with his best friend and his family.

## Library program
_Father’s Day brunch and book club._ Teens are encouraged to read the book alongside their fathers before the event. Four tables are set up, one for every Maghabol protagonist. At each table, a Filipino or American dish has been arranged with a printed passage from the book and discussion questions. Allow each table to discuss as a small group before sharing thoughts with the rest of the attendees.

## Speed-round book talk
What if you could hear your grandfather’s teenage thoughts, and your great-grandfather’s before that? _Everything We Never Had_ lets us listen in on the Maghabol family, starting with Francisco, a 1930s Filipino migrant who lands in Watsonville, California and ending with Enzo, an anxious teenager stuck at home during the COVID-19 pandemic. Both humorous and poignant, the novel showed me how “the past is the present is the future.”

## Potential challenges
This novel describes how people of color experience racism, making it the kind of book that often gets challenged (American Library Association, n.d.). While it is fictional, the characters and plot are based on real people and events, such as Larry Itliong, Fermin Tobera, and the Watsonville riots (Jones, 2018).

## Reason for inclusion
I wanted to include a book focused on teen boys, who have unique experiences and needs (Parker & Hurst, 2025). I also wanted to include a book about the Filipino diaspora, which makes up about four percent of the California population (Im, 2025; U.S. Census Bureau; n.d.). This book fit the bill on both counts, and as a bonus, it discusses mental health among Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders, who are less likely to seek professional mental health care than other teens (Huff, 2023).
