## Interest level
Ages 17 and up. TV-MA, per Netflix.

## Plot summary 
It’s 2075 in Night City, a dystopian California metropolis. Street violence is rampant, people are obsessed with cybernetic body modification, and a handful of megacorporations run everything from schools to hospitals. After 17-year-old David Martinez loses his mom and gets expelled from the prestigious Arasaka Academy, he survives the streets by becoming a mercenary — also known as an edgerunner.

This anime series extends the universe from video game _Cyberpunk 2077_, which itself was built on the _Cyberpunk_ tabletop game created by Mike Pondsmith in 1988 (Ostler, 2022). Studio Trigger is working on another standalone series, _Cyberpunk: Edgerunners 2_ (Dempsey, 2026).

## About the director
Hiroyuki Imaishi is a Japanese anime director known for _Gurren Lagann_ and _Promare_ (Anime News Network, n.d.) He worked at Gainax before founding Studio Trigger in 2011.

## Critical evaluation
_Cyberpunk: Edgerunners_ is fast-paced and thrilling. The dystopian universe danced with the limits of my fear, especially when its dystopian premise touched on our current reality. In a fictional 2075, corporations go to war to control healthcare; in real-world 2026, Americans face healthcare market consolidation and decreasing life expectancies (Malina et al., 2025). Night City residents pay for pleasurable “brain dances” that tax their bodies; real-world teens connect with friends through social media even though it hurts their mental health (Faverio et al., 2025).

I tolerated these technofeudalist horrors because despite them all, David and his friends try to take care of each other. The dark world of _Cyberpunk_ moved me to appreciate and fight for everything we haven’t yet lost. The electrifying original soundtrack helps, too.

## Library program
_Anime club._ I wouldn’t screen _Cyberpunk: Edgerunners_ at a library, especially not around younger high schoolers. But plenty of anime series are more appropriate and streamable on Netflix or Crunchyroll. Get a license, make some popcorn, and let teens relax with _Pokemon_, _Dragon Ball Z_, or _Frieren_.

## Speed-round book talk
In a futuristic and dystopian version of coastal California, people hack their bodies to keep up with capitalistic demands, to defend themselves against gang violence, or just to experience a few minutes of pleasure. When 17-year-old David Martinez and his mom get caught in a street accident, emergency responders see that they can’t pay for medical services and leave them hanging. The anime series follows David as he tries to survive these harsh conditions as an “edgerunning” outlaw.

## Potential challenges
The most graphically explicit work in this collection, _Cyberpunk: Edgerunners_ is susceptible to challenges. I would defend the series on the grounds that teens deserve access to content that helps them understand and process complex, difficult realities. The series depicts extreme behavior in a bleak world, but characters experience natural consequences for their actions. They also love and take care of one another. I’d recommend that concerned parents speak with their teens, but nothing in _Cyberpunk_ merits removal from the library.

## Reason for inclusion
Anime and dystopia are both very popular with teens. I wanted to include this exploration of capitalism, tech, and mental health through a genre that helps teens process big emotions (Nadworny, 2017). I also included the series because it has an upcoming standalone sequel (Dempsey, 2026).
