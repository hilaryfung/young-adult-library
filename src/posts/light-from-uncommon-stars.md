## Reading level and interest level
Age 17 and up. While the novel was written for adults, the teenage main character’s coming-of-age story has special appeal for young adults.

## Plot summary
Katrina Nguyen escapes an abusive home with a getaway bag and her beloved violin. She lands in San Gabriel Valley, where renowned violin teacher Shizuka Satomi takes her in. Shizuka offers to coach Katrina into the highest levels of violin success, and she seems to know exactly how to nurture Katrina as she steps fully, proudly, into her transgender identity. The catch? Katrina has to pay with her soul. Years ago, Shizuka made a deal with the devil, and she’s already condemned six young violin stars to hell. She owes one more, and she wants it to be Katrina’s.

While negotiating with the devil, Shizuka develops a budding romance with Lan Tran, who appears to be a Vietnamese immigrant running a doughnut shop, but is actually a starship captain seeking refuge from intergalactic warfare and an empire-destroying “endplague.” Lan is charmed by Shizuka but appalled that she would make such a horrific deal with the devil. Can her advanced space technology be used against a demon, who has supernatural powers of his own?

## Author background
Ryka Aoki is a poet, composer, teacher, and novelist (Aoki, n.d.). She wrote _Seasonal Velocities,_ a collection of poems, stories, and essays; _He Mele A Hilo,_ a novel of magical realism; and the poetry collection _Why Dust Shall Never Settle Upon This Soul._ A two-time finalist for the Lambda Literary Prize, she also won Lambda Literary’s prize for outstanding mid-career novelists (Lewis, 2023).

“I don’t think we had so much queer literature when I was growing up,” she said in an interview (Henderson, 2023). “Definitely not trans Asian-American literature! That’s one reason why, no matter what I write, be it poetry or essays or songs or science fiction novels, I bring my authentic self — even if it is in outer space with donuts — to the page.” 

## Critical evaluation
This novel warmed my heart. The plot is all over the place, but the maximalism works. Aoki’s descriptions are so rich, so sensory, that her love for violin music and San Gabriel Valley restaurants leap off the page. ChatGPT could never describe the human experience with this much color.

The characters and relationships are just as strong. Shizuka mothers Katrina gently but firmly. Shizuka and Lan develop a slow-burn romance, hesitantly revealing their baggage and finding tenderness at every turn. Each character makes mistakes — big ones, at that — but they express remorse and show each other grace. They show us how love is built.

## Library program
_Sound Director For A Day._ Although a film adaptation has been mentioned (Tu, 2021), it has not yet been made, which means a soundtrack to this novel has not been released. Select 10 scenes from the book. With a group of teens, ideally including some who have read the book, choose a song or sound that should accompany each scene. Be prepared with a list of songs, including classical violin pieces and tracks from Aoki’s own [playlist](https://open.spotify.com/playlist/5cQKLP7dCJcp2gY1sQYl4c), but allow teens to select songs they know and like, too. 

At the end, “perform” your new film by having teens read snippets from the book while your new soundtrack plays. Serve doughnuts, of course.

## Speed-round book talk
Life is harsh, and _Light From Uncommon Stars_ doesn’t shy away from that reality. But while they’re combatting abuse, transphobia, demons, and a multi-galaxy plague, the characters in this book enjoy many pleasures. A fresh doughnut, a steaming bite of Hainan chicken. A perfectly timed clapback, delivered while you’re on stage in a fabulous dress. Feeding ducks at the lake with your crush. Read this book if you liked _Everything Everywhere All At Once;_ read this book if you like romantic fantasy and well-written trans characters; read this book if you want to feel joy.

## Potential challenges
Transgender characters and depictions of the devil are common flash points in the world of book challenges. _Light From Uncommon Stars_ may be especially controversial because it discusses sex work. The book has been banned from one academic library in Maryland and one school library in Virginia on the basis of equity, diversity, and inclusion (EDI) content and transgender representation, respectively (American Library Association, 2026). 

Librarians can argue that overall, this novel provides a genuine depiction of transgender experience and coming of age; it’s not lurid or sensational. The publication of transgender stories is especially important for young people, whose physical and mental health are improved when they see themselves represented in popular media (Mocarski et al., 2019). Moreover, U.S. legal precedent stands on the side of creative freedom. In 1995, a federal district court in Kansas overturned a school district’s ban on a lesbian romance book (Glisten, n.d.).

Creative freedom also applies to the fantasy genre and depictions of the supernatural activity.

## Reason for inclusion
My reasons for inclusion are not unrelated to the potential challenges mentioned above. Aoki’s authentic portrayals of sex work and transgender coming of age are special. And it’s no exaggeration to say that this book made me feel alive. In an era when teens report high rates of anxiety, depression, and suicidality — transgender teens especially (Nath et al., 2026) — that’s a crucial feat.
