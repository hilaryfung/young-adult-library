## Interest level
All ages.

## Summary 
Players control cars, and cars play soccer. That’s basically it. You can boost your car’s rocket power by running over markers on the field. You can drive the gigantic ball toward your opponent’s goal or attack other cars, playing defense to give your teammates a chance to score. 

Standard gameplay puts three players on each team, but duels and doubles are on offer, too. You can play with someone next to you on a split screen. Players earn points as they progress, gaining titles like Expert, Legend, and All-Star at certain milestones. Experience also determines who you get matched with.

Epic Games, the studio that bought the original game developer Psyonix in 2019 (Statt, 2019), recently announced a new game engine, Unreal Engine 6, which will lend enhanced detail and lighting to a future version of _Rocket League_ (Franzese, 2026). Even though the game’s been out for years, developers are still improving it, and the community of players is active.

## Developer background
Psyonix was founded in 2001 in Raleigh, N.C. (Statt, 2019). Epic Games, known for _Fortnite_, bought the studio in 2019.

## Critical evaluation
It took me a bit to get used to the mechanics: navigating my car around the stadium and jumping to touch the ball. But I’m a novice when it comes to videogames, so if you’ve touched anything newer than a first-generation PlayStation, you’ll probably learn more quickly than I did. And I still had a lot of fun! Within my first half hour, my movements got more nimble, and I scored my first goal. I could see myself practicing dribbles, shots, and powerslides and enjoying the whole experience.

Playing on a friend’s PlayStation 5, I found the graphics and haptics satisfying, but the real fun is in the competition and the community. You can chat with friends and strangers in a voice channel, browse training packs created by other players, and participate in tournaments like the Season 23 World Cup, which runs through September (Rocket League, 2026). 

## Library program
_Rocket League World Cup_. Host a videogame tournament with international flair. Decorate the library with flags of and a world map highlighting countries that played in the most recent World Cup. Allow teens to choose countries to represent, starting with eight or 16 countries, depending on the number of participants. If a lot of teens show up, you can ask them to form teams, or run a round of 32. _Rocket League_ matches last five minutes with possible overtime, so once you get going, you can run about 10 matches in an hour.

Serve snacks representing a few of the countries. Teens can peruse a book display about soccer and cars, but they’ll likely want to spectate the whole time. This program is primarily a fun way to draw teens to the library, but it also teaches a bit of world geography and sportsmanship.

## Speed-round book talk
Personally, all I needed to hear was that you’re a car playing soccer. If you need more convincing, think state-of-the art graphics, social features, and fast-paced sports action. _Rocket League_ is easy to learn, but its challenges scale infinitely, so — as with the beautiful game itself — the fun never ends.

## Potential challenges
I don’t foresee challenges. Teens can talk to strangers in the game, so they should practice online safety.

## Reason for inclusion
_Rocket League_ is a simple, popular, and entertaining way for teens to decompress, whether they play alone or with friends.
