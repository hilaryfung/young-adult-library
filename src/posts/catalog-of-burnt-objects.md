## Awards and honors
None as of publication.

## Reading level and interest level
Ages 12 and up, according to [the publisher](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/673104/a-catalog-of-burnt-objects-by-shana-youngdahl/). 

## Plot summary 
_A Catalog of Burnt Objects_ opens on the day Caprice’s older brother comes home from rehab. Caprice is trying to trust him — every addiction recovery website says she should — but she’s wary. She doesn’t want him near the fortress of dreams that she’s built for herself. She’s secured local funding to build an app about her beloved hometown, Sierra, CA. She’s trying to use it to get a scholarship, get into college, and land a job in tech. Oh, and she hopes to go out with River, the cute new neighbor who runs past her house every day, even in record-breaking heat.

Then, wildfire tears through Sierra. Caprice’s dreams go up in smoke, along with her actual home. Her aunt’s flower shop, River’s moms’ new home, her best friend Alicia’s piano — everyone she knows loses almost everything they own. Worst of all, nobody knows where her Gramps went, or her cat Cheerio.

Time, in Caprice’s mind, starts to revolve around the fire. Two months before, three hours after, four weeks after that. The novel is structured the same way, and each chapter opens with a memory of an object, written from the perspective of the Sierra resident who lost it.

## Author background
Shana Youngdahl is a poet, professor, and author of the young adult novel _As Many Nows As I Can Get_ (Youngdahl, 2025). She’s from Paradise, CA, a small town in the Sierra Nevada foothills that was devastated by wildfire in 2018.

## Critical evaluation
This book didn’t quite land for me. Running jokes about garden gnomes and vaquitas went on for too long. I liked the computer programming language as an analogy for Caprice’s black-and-white thinking, but Youngdahl relied on it too much. Her characters all sounded the same, which might make sense in a small town, but the monotony made Youngdahl’s attempts at diversity feel like paint on factory-made characters purchased at Costco.

Some of the most poignant scenes are near the end, when Caprice finally goes back to town. She forces herself to revisit the hours she spent driving out of the smoke, the impossible decisions she made while her heart pounded through her chest. <span class="spoiler">She’s torn back to the present when she hits her head, and then her brother — an alcoholic holding a bottle of cinnamon-flavored whiskey — shows up to save her.</span>

I related in these moments to the people on the page, broken and trying to save one another. Many readers felt the same way, whether they’d seen addiction in their families (Nikki, 2025) or fire in their hometowns (Moore, 2025). Youngdahl draws on research about rehab, post-traumatic growth, and computer technology, as well as from her own life in Paradise, which is why the lack of detail in some passages feels so odd. It’s as if Youngdahl obscured certain scenes to protect real people, places, and flaws — and we can see the redaction marks.

## Library program
_Good fire._ For over a century, California outlawed cultural burns, resulting in densely packed, highly flammable landscapes (Schelenz, 2022). Youngdahl doesn’t mention this in _A Catalog of Burnt Objects,_ but a good library program should. Host a picnic and discuss the policies that shape your community’s risk and response to wildfire. You might refer to Redbud Resource Group, which provides a [classroom guide about cultural burns](www.redbudresourcegroup.org/post/teaching-tool-a-classroom-guide-to-cultural-burning-good-fire) (Baratta, 2025), or the podcast <a href="{{ '/all-my-relations/' | url }}">_All My Relations_</a>_,_ which includes an [episode about the Los Angeles wildfires](https://www.allmyrelationspodcast.com/podcast/episode/7e076e43/native-land-is-burning) of 2025.

## Speed-round book talk
Seventeen-year-old Caprice Alexander’s life hangs in a delicate balance. Her older brother hasn’t broken anything since he got home from rehab, and she’s working up the nerve to talk to the cute new guy who runs past her house every day. Then, wildfire destroys the small California town that three generations of her family have called home. This poignant novel explores trauma, grief, coping mechanisms, and the relationships that hold us together when everything else burns down.

## Potential challenges
The novel depicts a lesbian relationship and an interracial one. Several characters rely on marijuana to cope with hardship, and Caprice’s brother mentions meth, but he doesn’t use it, as far as readers can tell. That said, drugs and sex are not described, just mentioned, and the potential consequences of both are made clear. I can’t think of any passages that would raise serious objections.

## Reason for inclusion
As I write this, wildfire burns 30 miles from my home in Sunol, CA. For the foreseeable future, fire will be a regular part of California residents’ lives. This book discusses the impacts of climate disaster, exploring trauma, grief, and addiction through a relatable coming-of-age story.
