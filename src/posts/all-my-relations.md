## Interest level
Age 14 and up.

## Plot summary 
_All My Relations_ is a podcast about Native life. Guests are artists, activists, educators, elected officials, tribal leaders, and more. They talk about big, timely issues like immigration enforcement, hyperscale data centers, and missing and murdered Indigenous people. A lot of episodes are about culture; the podcast has hosted writer Julian Brave NoiseCat and the _Reservation Dogs_ creator Sterlin Harjo. The hosts sometimes get personal, discussing fertility, polyamory, and reconnection with one’s Indigenous community.

All My Relations also hosts a [book club](https://www.allmyrelationspodcast.com/book-club) in partnership with NDN Girls Book Club.

## About the hosts
_All My Relations_ is hosted by Matika Wilbur and Temeryss Xeli’tia Lane (All My Relations, n.d.).

Wilbur (Swinomish and Tulalip) founded Project 562 to photograph and share the stories of Native people from every tribe in the United States. Her photographs were published in _Project 562: Changing the Way We See Native America_. She opened Tidelands, a Seattle gallery and cultural hub, in 2024.

Xeli’tia Lane (Lummi Nation) is an athlete and the Vice President of Indian Country at Pyramid Communications, a Seattle-based strategy firm.

Adrienne Keene (Cherokee Nation) and Desi Small-Rodriguez (Northern Cheyenne and Chicana) previously hosted the show.

## Critical evaluation
I don’t know why it took me so long to listen to _All My Relations._ It’s funny and conversational, informative and up-to-date. “Extractive colonialism is the idea that y'all left but you left a big mess behind,” Keene says in the pilot episode, effortlessly answering Wilbur’s big question about what colonialism means. “In settler colonialism, it's y'all never left.”

Later in Season 1, a two-part series on blood quantum demonstrates the podcast’s range. In the first part, a molecular biologist tells us why ancestry can’t be determined from DNA, and a lawyer tells us about the indigenous people he’s defended from tribal disenrollment. The second part is an emotional conversation among women who want to love freely but are forced to consider blood quantum if they want to have children.

The hosts do a lot of teaching, but they also learn in public, displaying a graceful balance of vulnerability and wisdom. It’s everything I want in a podcast.

## Library program
_Local relations._ Indigenous culture is place-based, and the best way to program the podcast at your library is to find a local connection. See if _All My Relations_ has hosted a guest near you. Play the episode, or another piece of media about your community’s first peoples, and discuss with teens. Identify a recipe in a Native cookbook from your collection that you can prepare, either in advance of the program or with patron participation.

## Speed-round book talk
What does Indigenous resistance have to do with ICE? Do federal DEI policies affect tribal colleges and universities? Why is humor such a big part of Native culture? Whether you’re Native or not, listen to _All My Relations_ to understand the biggest issues affecting Indigenous people today.

## Potential challenges
Indigenous media is often targeted for banning (Cala et al., 2024). _All My Relations_ is likely to be no different, especially because it discusses intersecting issues such as immigration, sexuality, and environmental justice. The podcast deserves to be defended, as it is a critical tool for teens to hear new perspectives and understanding contemporary society.

## Reason for inclusion
California is home to more than 100 tribal communities (California Tribal Communities, n.d.). This podcast is an accessible tool for both Native and non-Native teens to learn about Indigenous life.
