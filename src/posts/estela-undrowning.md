Grades 10 to 12, per NoveList.

## Plot summary 
Right before her first day of senior year at San Francisco's ultra-competitive Robert Frost High School, Estela receives a letter from her landlord's daughter. Now, she’s staring down a possible eviction alongside four AP classes. She starts getting panic attacks, which get even worse when she places second in a Latiné Heritage Poetry Contest behind a student who’s not even Latino. Steli, as her friends call her, has to find strength — to “undrown” herself — under a floodlight she never asked for.

Estela gets by with the support of her family, her close friends Jeli Martínez and Chloe Ma, and a budding maybe-romance with her Spanish tutor, fellow student Rogelio Chang. Her story is told in prose scattered with journal entries, poems, and text messages. Spanish dialogue is woven in naturally, but English readers will understand as much as they need.

## Author background
René Peña-Govea is a Chicana writer, musician, and editor (Peña-Govea, 2026). Born and raised in San Francisco, she attended Lowell High School, the highly selective public school that inspired this debut novel.

## Critical evaluation
I thought _Estela, Undrowning_ was going to be about our main character swimming against racist currents at her school. And yes, she does confront the Chinese American student who takes the cash prize intended for Latinos and the Spanish teacher who dismisses heritage speakers in favor of textbook European grammar. But what I really loved was the complex internal conflict that Estela has to wade through. 

Even with an activist Chicana mother, friends who hold her through panic attacks, and a supportive new teacher — really the entire Mission District behind her — Steli still tries to go it alone. She tries to outperform racist stereotypes, and she distances herself from other Latina students who got into Frost not by testing in, like she did, but through the lottery. Peña-Govea does a fantastic job with Estela’s voice. Seventeen through most of the book, Steli is brilliant and loyal, glowing with desire and ambition, but she’s also tunnel-visioned and insecure. The author portrays Steli’s anxiety and selfishness with compassion, as symptoms of a larger pressure cooker, of the racial capitalism woven into the fabric of Steli’s high school and through society beyond.

Asian Americans aren’t the center of the book, but they make up about half of the student body at Lowell (California Department of Education, n.d.), the real school that inspired Estela’s fictional Frost. Readers get a nuanced look at an Asian community torn over lottery admissions, affirmative action, and hate-motivated assault. Black, multiracial, and queer characters play distinct roles, too, as they would in the San Francisco I know. Estela learns to find pride and joy in the collective, and in doing so, she gets stronger. Through every turn of plot, _Estela, Undrowning_ pulses with music, poetry, visual art, fashion design, and even bread — this story of Black, Asian, and Latino solidarity includes plenty of Mexican conchas and Hong Kong-style cocktail buns.

## Library program
_Jeli’s costume workshop._ Before Halloween, gather fabric, cardboard, and other craft materials, and invite teens to make costumes at the library. If your building has a sewing machine, demonstrate a basic stitch or two. If not, there are plenty of costumes you can make with hand sewing and glue. Get creative, and encourage teens to make climate-inspired costumes like Chloe does in the book.

## Speed-round book talk
It’s senior year, and all Estela wants to do is get through her extra-competitive high school and get into Cal with her best friend Jeli. But San Francisco doesn’t go easy on her. Estela’s family faces possible eviction from their longtime Mission District home, and she gets tied up in a scandal when she places second in the Latiné Heritage Poetry Contest — behind a Chinese American student. Can Estela defend her family, friends, and community while achieving her own dreams?

## Potential challenges
_Estela, Undrowning_ stakes its ground on interracial solidarity and pride, concepts that the Trump administration has attacked as “divisive equity ideology” (Markham & Friedman, 2026). The book also has more than a couple queer characters. Librarians should defend against challenges, which are authoritarian power grabs, not genuine attempts to protect young people.

## Reasons for inclusion
Students today face immense pressure to perform in high school, leading to anxiety, depression, and even suicidality (Steare et al., 2023). The pressure is intensified for students of color, low-income students, and those attending “high-achieving schools” like Estela’s (Luthar et al., 2020). The book addresses these issues, providing a model for how students might “undrown” themselves.

I also included this book because it shows how racism persists, even in diverse, progressive places, which many California cities are. Estela’s story of internalized racism and competition felt true and relevant for today’s teens.
