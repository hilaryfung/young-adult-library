## Summary 
For the past three years, Oakland-based non-profit Chapter 510 has supported teens in making a podcast. One season was about the high school experience, one was about the future, and the most recent season was about mental health and wellness. 

Teens interview each other, their parents, and their teachers. Sometimes they conduct street interviews or more specialized interviews. One episode is in Spanish; one is a live interview with author Nnedi Okorafor. Episodes are about 10 or 15 minutes long.

## About the creators
Chapter 510 is a non-profit organization and youth writing center in Oakland, CA (Oakland Literacy Coalition, n.d.). Oakland high schoolers in a Chapter 510 program work together to create the podcast.

## Critical evaluation
_Sound Space_ feels authentic. I’m not a teen, so I can’t say authoritatively whether it really feels like it’s made for teens, but I did get the sense that the speakers and interviewers were speaking to each other, not creating the show to win awards or beef up their college applications. I could hear Toussaint’s passion as he [interviewed a fellow “motorhead”](https://creators.spotify.com/pod/profile/chapter510/episodes/Petrol--Hobbies-and-Mental-Health---I-Hear-You-e3iovmm/a-ackke6d) about hobbies, and Lavender’s curiosity in an [interview with a Berkeley Free Clinic therapist](https://creators.spotify.com/pod/profile/chapter510/episodes/The-Price-of-Mental-Health---I-Hear-You-e3iovc6/a-ackkdnu) who spoke about healthcare in an “ideal world.”

The reporting isn’t particularly polished. In one episode, Maritza asked people on the street whether homelessness in Oakland has increased or decreased, and the editor in my head begged for factual context. On the same episode, though, she interviews her mom, who spent a chapter of her life without secure housing. The podcast is best in these personal moments, where teens report on issues they really care about. From [Jen](https://creators.spotify.com/pod/profile/chapter510/episodes/Domestic-vs--International-Studies---Tales-of-Surviving-High-School-e2jceg4), an immigrant from Vietnam, I learned about a teacher who uses Google Translate to accommodate international students but who worries the practice conflicts with the classroom no-phone policy. One of my favorite episodes was [Zea’s](https://creators.spotify.com/pod/profile/chapter510/episodes/Unfiltered-Real-Talk-on-Sex-Education---Tales-of-Surviving-High-School-e2jceis). After advocating for sexual education at her Catholic high school with mixed success, Zea interviewed one of her more supportive school directors about what sex ed means, and about how _she_ experienced sex ed when she was younger.

## Library program
_Podcast circle._ With contemporary technology, podcasts can come together surprisingly quickly. Listen to a few episodes of _Sound Space’s_ [2024 season](https://www.chapter510.org/tales) with a group of teens. Then, brainstorm topics in the theme of surviving high school. Identify the group’s favorite topic, then break teens into pairs, or groups of three if necessary. Ask each pair to create a one- to three-minute recording on the topic. At the end, seat teens in a circle and play each recording for a live “podcast” experience.

## Speed-round ~~book~~ podcast talk
In _Sound Space_, teens talk about the issues really on their minds. One high schooler asks if robots will take everyone’s jobs by the time they graduate. Another explores the relationship between grades, tests, and genuine learning. From mental resilience to internet queer culture to bullying, every episode delivers a short, cleanly produced nugget on a relevant topic.

## Potential challenges
The podcast covers sex and mental illness, so it might attract challenges. But teens choose the topics on the podcast, so the argument that adults are passing mature themes into youth media really won’t fly here.

## Reason for inclusion
When I set out to make this collection, I was certain that I wanted some form of youth-led media in the mix. This podcast turned out to be the best choice. The format lends itself to experimentation and allows teen voices to shine. And while many items in the collection explore mental health through fiction, I wanted some material that addresses the topic directly. This podcast does that, covering timely issues in a fresh and honest way.
